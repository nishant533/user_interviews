3 Personas · MGA Assignment 4 · Interviewed by Nishant · April 2026

Rahul Anand

Basic
Customer Support Agent SaaS Product Apr 28, 2026 Interviewed by Nishant
Snapshot

Rahul is an AI-aware professional who uses LLMs at a basic, occasional level — primarily to refine customer support responses and for self-learning outside work. He knows agentic AI exists and recognizes AI fluency is becoming a mandatory career skill, but hasn't moved beyond single-prompt usage. He's stuck not from lack of awareness, but from time poverty and not having defined a concrete problem to solve.

Current AI Usage
  • ChatGPT (work) + Gemini (personal paid) — tool choice driven by access, not strategy
  • "From time to time, not every day" — refines tone of support responses, asks for de-escalation strategies
  • Gemini Deep Research → NotebookLM for mind maps and simplified versions
  • Stays AI-aware via YouTube channels
  • Has never built any automation, flow, or multi-step AI workflow
Key Quotes
"Right now I just use it at the very minimal level. I don't completely make use of it."
"After a while, you feel like the responses are like a tool response… it always gives a generic response, which is good enough in most cases."
"I think every company will look for prompt engineering in their employees as a basic skill."
"I'm just too exhausted to again sit and work on this."
Pain Points
⚙️ PracticalNo bandwidth — work hours are fully consumed; post-work he's "too exhausted to sit and work on this"
⚙️ PracticalDoesn't know HOW to wire automations — how to trigger a flow, get input into a message field, send automatically
⚙️ PracticalTutorial-scroller, not project-builder — "I just randomly scroll through tutorials… haven't sat by myself, defined a problem and tried to fix it"
🧠 PsychologicalAI feels like a black box — "I don't know exactly how it works… math and logic behind it… a bit difficult to grasp"
🧠 PsychologicalRecognizes career stakes but feels stuck — knows AI fluency will be mandatory, can't act on it
Aha Moments & Triggers
  • Realized AI fluency is becoming mandatory ("like how Excel used to be")
  • DeepMind's cancer research breakthroughs amazed him — registered as "wow" not "I could try this"
  • Has a clear use case in mind: AI knowledge base for SaaS support to handle "how-to" questions automatically — but doesn't know how to build it
Hypothesis Signals
ConfirmsTime poverty + absence of a concrete problem statement is the real bottleneck — not awareness, not tools, not motivation in theory
ChallengesThe assumption that "tutorials and content" close the gap — Rahul consumes them but they don't translate to action
New InsightTool selection at this level is purely driven by ease of access, not informed comparison — there's no "tool fluency" before "AI fluency"
Headline Insight
I know AI fluency will be a mandatory skill — but I'm too exhausted after work, and I haven't sat down to define a problem worth solving.
Open Questions for Round 2
  • Would a guided "starter project" framework actually break the plateau, or is the issue genuinely time/energy?
  • If his employer made AI exploration part of work hours, would behavior change?

Chandu Appasani

Intermediate
Tech Support Lead SaaS Product Apr 28, 2026 Interviewed by Nishant
Snapshot

Chandu is a former engineer turned support/operations leader who actively uses Claude across multiple workflows — task prioritization, analytics, content refinement, system prompt generation. He's built Zapier automations and uses curiosity-driven exploration ("like a kid with a new phone"). He wants to graduate from prompt-based usage to true agentic workflows but is throttled by his actual job: human handling and firefighting consume his exploration time.

Current AI Usage
  • Daily: Communication refinement, tone adjustment for customer messages
  • Built a daily prioritization workflow in Claude — feeds calendar + pending tasks, gets reprioritized list
  • Whisper voice → Claude prompts for time zone math and wait-time logic
  • Generates analytics from JIRA/CSAT dumps (with mixed success)
  • Used Claude to author a system prompt — turned a 7–8 hour task into 1 hour
  • Built Zapier flows for JIRA tracking, partnership round-robin, onboarding routing
Key Quotes
"It's helping me do things better, but is it doing things for me? That's the question I want to solve."
"I'm not trying to solve enough problems with AI."
"Cynthia's project amazed me… and also that upset me a lot. I should have got the idea and I should have implemented that."
"The fact that I'm not using AI enough — and I'm not getting time to explore — that frustrates me the most."
Pain Points
⚙️ PracticalJob is "too much firefighting" — human handling, escalations, agent backups eat all exploration time
⚙️ PracticalWhen automations break, debugging is expensive — has abandoned multiple JIRA flows after bugs
⚙️ PracticalGot stuck for 2 hours on a data analysis prompt, gave up without root-cause analysis
🧠 PsychologicalFOMO/jealousy when peers ship AI projects — "I should have got the idea"
🧠 Psychological"Leverage" gap — even when he has ideas, approval/budget friction blocks execution
⚙️ PracticalDoesn't always know whether a failure is the prompt, the model, or the data — model swap fixed one issue but he discovered that by accident
Aha Moments & Triggers
  • Podcast about "moving from prompt to agentic AI" — became his current learning direction
  • The 7–8 hour task done in 1 hour (Claude-generated system prompt) — visceral ROI moment
  • Got introduced to Zapier by a colleague — pure curiosity: "Bilkul nahi pata tha. Yeh kya hai? Dekhna hai mujhe."
  • Approach to new tools: "Like a kid with a new phone — every clickable option, I click on it"
Hypothesis Signals
ConfirmsFor intermediates, the blocker is TIME and the COST OF DEBUGGING when things break — not access, not awareness, not aspiration
ChallengesThe "tool overload" theory — Chandu doesn't want more tools, he wants more depth with the ones he already uses (Claude + Zapier)
New InsightSocial proof is double-edged — peer success can inspire OR demoralize. The reaction depends on whether you feel you have the leverage to act
Headline Insight
AI is helping me do things better — but I want it doing things for me. The block isn't tools or knowledge; it's that human handling at work consumes the time I'd need to build agents.
Open Questions for Round 2
  • How could "AI exploration" be re-framed as part of the job rather than extra to it?
  • What's the smallest agentic project he could ship in his current bandwidth that proves value?

Shaheen Hyder

Advanced
Software Engineer Zoco Apr 28, 2026 Interviewed by Nishant
Snapshot

Shaheen is a self-taught engineer (skipped college, learned via maker-space community + peer learning) who uses Claude Code and Codex daily as a thinking partner, not just a code generator. He's articulated a clear mental model for AI-fluent work: get context first, think on your own, discuss with AI, then have AI implement. He's reliability-first rather than experimentation-first — comfortable lagging the bleeding edge to ship dependable work.

Current AI Usage
  • Daily: Claude Code + Codex inside JetBrains IDE — picks model by task (UI → Claude, logic → Codex)
  • Recent project: 2-week onboarding flow change → completed in less than a day with AI
  • Workflow: entry point → AI walks through conditions → think alone → discuss with AI → AI implements → iterative debug
  • Now confidently builds frontend (was previously back-end only) — AI's UI taste fills his skill gap
  • Stays updated via WhatsApp peer groups, not aggressive experimentation
Key Quotes
"Even before solving, we should first just discuss [with the AI]. Usually we can find a better solution than solving initially, and then solve it."
"What we are doing now in two days, it would take kind of a month or so [without AI]."
"If we are asking AI to do many things in a go, it will get isolated… breaking down is a very good thing that we always have."
"I always prefer reliability more than that kind of optimal."
Pain Points
⚙️ PracticalStack Overflow declining — for novel/deep technical problems, neither AI nor human community has answers. Real dead-ends now exist
⚙️ PracticalAI breaks on complex multi-step tasks — has to break down problems and visually debug
🧠 PsychologicalMild trade-off cost from being reliability-first — "we will not realize a few things the earliest" (he's okay with this)
⚙️ PracticalClear AI-adoption gap in legacy companies (Infosys-type) — restrictions + cultural unawareness, not skill issue
Aha Moments & Triggers
  • Co-pilot autocomplete → Claude Code as IDE agent = clear evolution he can articulate
  • Realized AI handles edge cases better than humans even think to consider — shifted his trust
  • Discovered: when YOU lack a skill (e.g., UI design), AI's "default taste" can be better than yours
  • Maker-space community in 11th grade was the original unlock — exposure → curiosity → self-teaching
Hypothesis Signals
ConfirmsThe fluency gap is real and methodology-shaped — Shaheen has a mental model intermediates lack ("think first, discuss with AI, then implement")
New InsightThe unlock at advanced level isn't more tools or more prompts — it's a discipline of breaking work down and discussing before solving. This is tacit, rarely articulated
New InsightEven advanced users intentionally lag the bleeding edge if their goal is shipping. "Reliability vs. experimentation" is a stable preference, not a fluency level
ChallengesThe framing that advanced users have no frustrations — Shaheen has meta-level concerns (Stack Overflow's death, AI ceiling on truly novel problems)
Headline Insight
AI fluency isn't about copy-pasting code — it's about thinking with AI before letting it implement. The advanced users who struggle are the ones who skip the thinking step.
Open Questions for Round 2
  • Is the "think first, discuss with AI" methodology teachable, or does it need to be experienced?
  • What would an "AI fluency curriculum" look like for a non-engineer applying this same mental model?
